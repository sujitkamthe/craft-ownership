01 · Outside Voices

Client Communication

When your team ships to a client — a paying customer, an internal partner team, an external org — your communication is part of the product. The code can be excellent; a client left in the dark about what changed and why will still feel let down. Senior engineers own that experience alongside the code.

Key Insight

Clients don’t measure engineers primarily on code. They measure on predictability: do I know what’s coming, when, and what to expect if it slips? The engineer who delivers average code with clockwork communication earns more trust than the engineer who delivers brilliant code and goes silent for three weeks.

Habits that build trust with clients

The client-communication baseline

  • Predictable updates. Weekly or bi-weekly, same cadence, same format. Clients stop worrying when they can count on it.
  • Flag risks early, not late. The “no surprises” rule matters double for clients. A slip flagged two weeks out is a conversation; a slip flagged on the promised date is a crisis.
  • Translate tech to impact. “We refactored the payment module” means nothing to them. “We eliminated a class of bugs that caused 3 outages last quarter” means everything.
  • Respect their time. 20-minute meetings that could be a Slack message. 50-slide decks with one useful slide. Senior communication is brief.
  • Be present when it’s hard. After an incident, after a slip, after a push-back — show up, own it, own the fix. Easy communication in good times is worth little; honest communication in hard times earns years of trust.

Saying no to clients — with grace

Clients ask for things that don’t fit the plan. “Yes” to everything breaks the plan; blunt “no” breaks the relationship. Senior engineers find the honest middle: name the trade-off, give them the choice.

💬

Weak no: “We can’t do that.”

Strong no: “We can do that — but it means feature X slips two weeks, or we cut the edge-case handling we’d planned for Y. I’d lean against option 2 because we’d likely see an incident within a month. Which trade-off do you prefer?”

The strong version treats the client as a partner and gives them agency. You’ve also modelled the thinking so they can make the next call themselves.

Common pitfall

Going silent during a hard period. “We’re heads down; I’ll update when we have news.” Clients hear: “something’s wrong and they don’t want to say.” Trust drops fast during silence.

Better approach

Over-communicate through hard periods. “No news this week, still working through the unknowns we flagged last Friday, expect substantive update Monday.” Even “no news” is news when said on time.

👤

A working note on consulting contexts: if your team works at a client site or as an embedded partner, you are both a team member and a guest. The guest half means extra care with tone: suggest before insist, ask before assume, teach before critique. Over a year, this earns the kind of trust where clients start asking your opinion on things you weren’t even contracted for — which is where the most interesting work happens.

02 · Clarity

Structured Communication — Pyramid & SCR

Most engineering communication buries the important thing at the bottom of a long build-up. Structured communication inverts that: the conclusion comes first, the supporting logic follows, and the reader can stop reading when they’re satisfied. It’s the single highest-leverage skill for talking to busy stakeholders.

The Pyramid Principle

Lead with the answer; support it after

Barbara Minto’s Pyramid Principle: start with the main point, then give three or so supporting arguments, then the evidence under each argument. The reader always knows what you’re saying; they choose how deep to go.

The opposite — narrative order, background first, conclusion last — works for stories and wastes the reader’s time in business communication.

Narrative — conclusion last 
Last quarter we started seeing P95
latency drift. We investigated several
options. After benchmarking we tried
approach A, which didn't help. Then we
tried B, which helped but introduced
complexity. Eventually we landed on C.

Recommendation: adopt C.
Pyramid — conclusion first 
Recommendation: adopt C for P95 latency.

- C cuts P95 by 40% with minimal ongoing
  complexity.
- We ruled out A (no effect) and B (helpful
  but adds complexity we'd regret).
- Benchmark data, cost, and risk below.

[details follow]

SCR — Situation, Complication, Resolution

SCR is a lightweight version of the pyramid for short communications — emails, update posts, a 2-minute verbal update.

  • Situation: one line on what’s true / what we agreed.
  • Complication: what’s changed or broken.
  • Resolution: what you propose / what’s being done.

Three sentences. Works for a Slack update, a status post, a meeting opener. The reader knows in 15 seconds what they need to know.

📝

SCR example:

Situation: Payment migration was tracking to ship Friday.
Complication: Two edge cases surfaced in staging this morning that weren’t in the original scope.
Resolution: I’m extending by two days, shipping Monday. The edge cases are real; rushing Friday risks incident. I’ll send a fuller note at standup.

MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive

When you present options, the set should be MECE: each option distinct from the others (mutually exclusive), and together covering the real space (collectively exhaustive). “We could do A, B, or C” is useless if A and B overlap, or if D was the obviously-right option you forgot.

Check a list of options against MECE

  • Mutually exclusive: could the reader pick two of them and not be contradicting themselves?
  • Collectively exhaustive: is there a reasonable path the reader might ask about that’s missing? Usually “do nothing” or “wait and reassess” deserves to be in the set.
📚

Deeper reading: The Pyramid Principle by Barbara Minto is the original; dense but influential. For a short, modern treatment, read the “Pyramid Principle” section of The McKinsey Way. For day-to-day practice: writing your next Slack status update in SCR will teach you more than reading either.

03 · Written

Documentation People Actually Read

Most engineering documentation goes unread. The ones that get read share a few properties: they’re written for a specific reader, they answer the question the reader came with, and they’re kept alive — not preserved as monuments. Senior engineers treat documentation as craft, not a side-tax on the “real” work.

Key Insight

Write every document for a named reader doing a named task. “An engineer joining the project”, “an on-call responding to an alert”, “a stakeholder evaluating feasibility.” Generic documentation — written for nobody in particular — stays generically unread.

The documents worth writing well

README

The first thing a new reader sees. Must answer: what is this, how do I run it, where do I go next. If your make dev doesn’t work from a fresh checkout by following the README alone, the README is wrong — fix it before the next new-joiner hits it.

Architecture overview

One page with a diagram and a paragraph per major component. Helps a new engineer build a mental model in 15 minutes instead of three days. Update when shape changes; don’t let it lie.

Runbook

For an on-call under pressure. Name the symptom, give the diagnostic steps, list the top failure modes. See handover.

Onboarding guide

“Your first two weeks.” What to install, what to read, what small PR to aim for. Saves every future hire days of groping around.

Design doc

For a decision being weighed. Covered in requirements & design.

ADR

For a decision already made. Covered in requirements & design.

Post-mortem

After an incident. Covered in post-mortem practice.

How-to guides

Specific task-based docs: “How to add a new payment provider”, “How to roll a secret”. Written once, saves the team the Slack thread every time someone new needs to do the thing.

Writing docs that stay useful

Habits that keep docs alive

  • Write for the task, not the topic. “How to deploy” beats “Deployment system.” Task docs are read; topic docs are archived.
  • Put a “last reviewed” date at the top. Forces periodic re-read; signals to readers how fresh the content is.
  • One source of truth per topic. Four docs on deploying means readers find the wrong one and follow stale steps. Consolidate; link rather than duplicate.
  • Link rather than embed. If another doc already explains a prerequisite, link; don’t paste. When the original changes, the link stays current; the paste goes stale.
  • Delete docs that have rotted past repair. Out-of-date docs are worse than no docs — they mislead. Mark or remove them.
  • Review docs on PR that changed the underlying thing. “Does this PR require a doc update?” in your review checklist. Close the loop.
📝

Scenario: a team audits its docs folder. Half the pages are 2+ years old, contradicting each other, mentioning systems that have been deleted. They delete about 60% and consolidate the rest into six “canonical” pages with clear readers and last-reviewed dates. Readership of the remaining pages triples in the next quarter. Fewer, better docs beat more, stale ones.

Common pitfall

Writing comprehensive documentation in one big push when a service ships. Nobody reads the 40-page PDF; it ages quickly; the team quietly stops trusting docs in general.

Better approach

Write docs in small pieces, keyed to specific reader tasks. Update them as the code changes. Treat a PR that touches behavior but not its doc as half-done.

04 · Why

Product & Business Sense

Most engineers can build what they’re told. Senior engineers push further: they know why they’re building it, who benefits, what problem it solves, what success looks like. That understanding shapes better decisions at every step — from API design to how much test coverage is enough.

Key Insight

The engineer who asks “who is this for?” before coding often discovers that half the proposed scope is unnecessary, or that a simpler solution would better serve the real user. That discovery is senior-engineer value — unlocked by curiosity, not by more code.

Questions to ask yourself on every feature

  • Who is this for? Real users, not personas. If you can’t name three, do you actually know?
  • What problem is it solving? In one sentence. Not “the feature” — the underlying pain it removes.
  • What’s the evidence the problem matters? Support tickets, revenue impact, user research, competitive pressure. If no evidence, is the priority justified?
  • What happens if we don’t do it? Often the honest answer is “not much” — which is useful information.
  • What does success look like in a month? In a year? A number, a behaviour change, something measurable.
  • What’s the simplest version that would still work? Often smaller than the original proposal.

Partnering with stakeholders instead of taking orders

The best engineer-stakeholder relationships are partnerships. The stakeholder brings customer insight, market context, prioritisation judgment. The engineer brings feasibility, trade-offs, and a sense of what’s actually expensive. Together they shape the feature. Senior engineers show up to that partnership prepared — with questions, with alternatives, with honest costs.

Common pitfall — order-taker mode

“The ask was build X. I built X.” The feature ships, the stakeholder is happy in the short term, but the engineer never added value beyond execution. Over time, their scope stays narrow; the stakeholder stops asking for opinions.

Better approach — partner mode

“The ask was build X. I asked why, and what they’d measure. I proposed X-minus (smaller, faster) and X-plus (handles an edge case they hadn’t mentioned). We landed on X-minus for now, X-plus on the roadmap. I’ll know in 3 weeks if we need X-plus sooner.” The engineer shaped the outcome.

🚀

Watch the metrics after ship. Did users adopt it? Did the metric you predicted move? If yes, you learn something about what works; if no, you learn something important about what doesn’t. Either way, you’re now a better partner for the next feature. Most engineers ship and move on — and stay mediocre at product sense forever.

Translating engineering impact for the business

When you explain a piece of engineering work to stakeholders, translate at least once from tech-words to business-words. Not as dumbing down — as respect for their time and your shared goal.

Engineering-speak 
We refactored the ingestion pipeline
from a monolithic lambda to a set
of composable stages with typed
intermediaries and retry boundaries.
Translated for impact 
We rebuilt the data import so that
when one step fails, the rest keep
running. It's about 3x faster for
typical files, and we stopped losing
a day whenever the largest customer's
upload had a single bad row.

Pick one this week:

  • Rewrite your next status update in SCR (Situation-Complication-Resolution). Notice how much shorter it gets and how much faster the reader lands on what matters.
  • Open your project’s README. Try to set up the project on a fresh machine following only what’s written there. Fix every step that fails.
  • On your next feature, ask whoever owns the ask: “who is this for, what problem does it solve, and what would success look like in a month?” See what the conversation opens up.
