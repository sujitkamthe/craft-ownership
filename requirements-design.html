Requirements & Design

Understanding the real problem before you write a line. How to clarify what’s being asked, break it down, and capture the decisions that matter.

01 · Problem

Clarify the Real Problem

The cheapest bug to fix is the one you find before you start coding. The cheapest rework is the requirement you clarified before the design. Clarification is not a junior task you’re too senior for — it’s where the leverage is.

Key Insight

A Jira ticket is a suggestion, not a specification. The words on it are a compressed version of a conversation somebody had weeks ago. Your job is to decompress — find out what problem is actually being solved, for whom, and what “done” looks like in their eyes.

The 5 Whys on requirements

Before you start, answer these five

  1. Why is this being asked? — What’s the underlying user or business problem?
  2. Why now? — What changed that made this a priority? Is there a real deadline or an imagined one?
  3. Why this shape? — Is the proposed solution the right one, or has “we’ve always done it this way” locked in a weak design?
  4. Why can’t we do less? — What’s the smallest thing that would still make the user happier?
  5. Why is this our team’s work? — Is this the right layer of the stack? Is another team better placed?

You don’t need to ask all five out loud. You do need to have an answer to each before you start.

Edge cases before code

Spend 20 minutes listing failure modes before you implement. What happens on an empty list? A very large list? A duplicate submission? A slow network? An unexpected null? A user who refreshes mid-flow? A retry that arrives twice?

🔍

Most “edge case” bugs in production are cases the team forgot to think about, not cases they thought about and got wrong. Thinking is cheap. Forgetting is expensive.

“Done” in measurable terms

Before you start: can you state the acceptance criteria in one paragraph that a peer could verify without asking you anything? If not, you don’t yet understand the ask well enough to start.

02 · Communication

Asking Good Questions

Senior engineers ask better questions, not more of them. A well-framed question saves the PM an hour of back-and-forth and shows you’ve actually thought about the problem.

Low-effort questions

“What should happen if the input is invalid?” — The answer is always “show a helpful error.” You’re making the PM do the thinking for you.

High-effort questions

“On invalid input, my default would be: validate inline and block submit until resolved. Alternative would be allow submit, show errors on the next screen. The first is stricter but disrupts flow. Which do we want?”

The question template that respects everyone’s time

  1. State the question in one sentence.
  2. Share your default / recommendation.
  3. Share the alternative(s) you considered.
  4. State the trade-off as you see it.
  5. Ask for a decision.

If you can’t do steps 2–4 yet, you may be asking too early — think a bit more first.

🎯

Write your questions down as you work, not as they come up. Batch them. One thoughtful message with five questions is far kinder to a PM than five interruptions over a day.

03 · Planning

Breaking Features into Stories

A feature broken down well feels effortless to build and easy to show progress on. A feature broken down poorly becomes a 3-week mystery with a big demo at the end.

Vertical slices over horizontal layers

Horizontal slicing

Story 1: DB schema. Story 2: API. Story 3: UI. Story 4: integration. You can’t demo anything until all four are done; the feature exists in pieces nobody can use.

Vertical slicing

Story 1: happy path end-to-end, minimal UI, one record only. Story 2: validation + errors. Story 3: edit & delete. Story 4: pagination + search. Every story ends with something you can demo.

The INVEST heuristic for stories

  • Independent — each story can ship on its own schedule where possible.
  • Negotiable — the scope can move without re-writing the story.
  • Valuable — on its own, it does something a user or operator would notice.
  • Estimable — you can size it without a full day of investigation.
  • Small — fits comfortably in a sprint, ideally a few days.
  • Testable — there’s a clear way to verify done.

Parallelisation — so the team isn’t bottlenecked on you

When you slice, think about who on the team can pick up which slice. If every story goes through your hands sequentially, you are the bottleneck. A good breakdown has stories that two or three people can work in parallel.

📈

Why this matters: Features that parallelise well finish 30–50% faster and distribute knowledge across the team. The breakdown is not busywork — it’s the schedule.

04 · Artifact

Lightweight Design Docs

A design doc is a short written sketch of how you’ll build something, shared before you start. It’s not a thesis, and it’s not an approval gate. It’s a cheap way to catch design mistakes while they still cost minutes, not weeks.

When to write one

  • The feature touches more than two services or two major modules.
  • A reasonable engineer could disagree with your approach.
  • There’s a non-trivial trade-off (performance vs. simplicity, consistency vs. availability, etc.).
  • The work will take more than a week to build.
  • You want structured feedback before you commit.

For a one-story change with no ambiguity, a design doc is overhead. Skip it.

A design doc template that fits on two pages

  1. Context — what problem, for whom, why now.
  2. Goals & non-goals — what this will and won’t do. Non-goals are as important as goals.
  3. Proposed design — the approach, a sketch diagram, the data model, the API shape.
  4. Alternatives considered — the two or three other things you thought about and why you didn’t pick them.
  5. Trade-offs & risks — what you’re giving up, what could go wrong.
  6. Rollout & observability — how it ships, how you’ll know it’s working.
  7. Open questions — what you want reviewers to weigh in on.

Two pages. Written in 2–3 hours. Reviewed in 30 minutes. Saves days.

📝

Share the doc for review before you’ve fallen in love with the design. If you’ve already coded half of it, the feedback comes in as “too late” and reviewers stop being honest.

05 · Artifact

ADRs — When a Decision Deserves a Record

An Architecture Decision Record is a one-page note that captures a significant technical decision: what you chose, why, and what you ruled out. The future reader — often future-you — will thank you.

When to write an ADR

Not for every decision. Write one when:

  • The decision is hard to reverse (a database choice, a protocol, a vendor lock-in).
  • The decision will surprise a future reader who doesn’t know the history.
  • You considered alternatives that might look obvious later but weren’t available then.
  • The decision was made after real debate, and you want to preserve the conclusions.

ADR template (one page)

  1. Title — a short statement of the decision (“Use Postgres JSONB for event payload storage”).
  2. Status — proposed, accepted, superseded, deprecated.
  3. Context — what situation forced the decision.
  4. Decision — what was chosen, in plain language.
  5. Consequences — the good, the bad, and the “we’ll have to revisit this if.”
  6. Alternatives considered — what else was on the table.

Key Insight

The test of a good ADR isn’t how it reads today. It’s whether, 18 months from now, someone wondering “why did we do it this way?” can get their answer in a minute. If they can, you’ve saved the team days of re-debate.

06 · Risk

Spike vs. Build

When the work has a real unknown — a library you haven’t used, a performance question you can’t answer by reasoning, an integration with an unfamiliar system — the right first move is often a timeboxed spike, not a full build.

What a good spike looks like

The spike contract

  • Question — one specific unknown you’re answering.
  • Timebox — half a day, one day, two days. Name it.
  • Output — a short write-up or a throwaway prototype, not production code.
  • Stopping rule — when the time is up, you stop, even if the answer is only 70% certain. Then you decide.
Common pitfall

The spike quietly becomes the implementation. Prototype code gets promoted to production “because it already works.” You inherit its shortcuts forever.

Better approach

When the spike answers the question, stop. Throw away the prototype code (or mark it “spike — do not promote”). Start the real build fresh, informed by what you learned.

⏱️

If a spike is running past its box by more than 50%, pause and ask why. Usually the question was too big. Split it: answer the narrower sub-question first.

07 · Commitment

Estimation without Over-Committing

Estimation is a commitment with a confidence band. Senior engineers give honest estimates including the risk, not optimistic numbers they know they won’t hit.

Three estimates, not one

The 3-point estimate

  • Best case — if everything goes right, the environment behaves, and there are no surprises.
  • Realistic case — the number you’d actually bet on, knowing this team and this codebase.
  • Worst case — if known risks materialise, but not catastrophes.

A one-number estimate lies by omission. Three numbers tell the truth: here is the range, and here is what moves it.

The 1.5× buffer rule

For any feature where you’re touching code you don’t know well, integrating with a system you haven’t integrated with before, or solving a problem you haven’t solved before — multiply your gut estimate by 1.5. That is the one you commit to. The original is your best case, not your expected case.

Key Insight

The cost of missing an estimate is not just the slippage — it’s the trust hit. Stakeholders remember the engineer who said “two weeks” and took five. Stakeholders also remember the engineer who said “three weeks, maybe four” and delivered in three and a half. Predictability beats optimism.

Common pitfall

Estimating only the coding time. The review round-trips, the test writing, the integration work, the unknowns, the meetings, the context-switching — all of those live outside “coding time” and together often double the real duration.

Better approach

Estimate the complete path to shipped: design, build, test, review cycles, integration, deploy, observe. That’s the number you owe the team.

Reference stories

The fastest way to improve estimates is to build a small mental library of reference stories: “that payments refactor was X days, this is similar shape, therefore similar order of magnitude.” You’ll be wrong less often than by guessing from scratch.

Pick one this week:

  • Before your next meaningful story, run the “5 Whys on requirements” with your PM — even informally. See what shifts.
  • Write a lightweight two-page design doc for something you were about to code directly. Share it for review before you start.
  • On your next estimate, give three numbers (best / realistic / worst) instead of one. Notice how the conversation with your leader or PM changes.
